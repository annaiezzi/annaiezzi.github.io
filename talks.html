  • 06/01/2022: Finding cycles in supersingular isogeny graphs, Combinatorics 2022, Mantova, Italy.

    Finding cycles in supersingular isogeny graphs

    Supersingular isogeny graphs are graphs whose vertices correspond to (isomorphism classes of) supersingular elliptic curves over finite fields and whose edges represent (equivalence classes of) isogenies between elliptic curves. A cycle in such graphs corresponds to an isogeny from a supersingular curve $E$ (of the cycle) to itself, i.e. to an endomorphism of $E$. Computing non-trivial endormorphisms of a supersinguar elliptic curve , i.e. finding cycles in a supersingular isogeny graph, is a computationally difficult problem which underlies the hard problem of computing the endomorphism ring of a supersingular el- liptic curve over a finite field.

    A motivation for this computational problem comes from cryptography, and more precisely from a young subfield of post-quantum cryptography called isogeny-based cryptography. The security of an isogeny-based cryptosystem is based on the mathematical problem of computing an isogeny between two elliptic curves $E$ and $E_0$ and it has been shown that, in the supersingular case, this problem can be reduced to the computation of the endomorphism rings of $E$ and $E_0$.

    In this talk, after reviewing the mathematical and cryptographic context, we will present an improved algorithm for computing the endomorphism ring of a supersingular elliptic curve over a finite field.

  • 09/22/2021: Bruhat–Tits trees as a cryptanalitic tool for isogeny-based cryptography, First Annual Conference on Cryptography and Coding Theory, organised by the Unione Matematica Italiana (UMI) and the initiative De Componendis Cifris (online).

    Bruhat–Tits trees as a cryptanalitic tool for isogeny-based cryptography

    Isogeny-based cryptography is a young and very active subfield of post-quantum cryptography. It could be considered as a new kind of elliptic-curve cryptography, since, as the name suggests, it is based on the notion of isogeny, i.e. a non-constant morphism between elliptic curves which is also a group homomorphism.

    Supersingular isogeny graphs are one of the main characters of this kind of cryptography. These graphs, whose vertices correspond to (isomorphism classes of) supersingular elliptic curves over finite fields and whose edges represent (equivalence classes of) isogenies between elliptic curves, have not been fully explored yet, and actually their ``unknown" structure underlies the security of some promising isogeny-based cryptosystems such as SIKE (a 3rd round alternate candidate for the NIST competition).

    Thanks to Deuring's correspondence, supersingular isogeny graphs are normally studied from the algebraic point of view of quaternion algebras, where supersingular elliptic curves are translated into maximal orders and isogenies into linking ideals. In this talk we propose that we add to the game another tool of number-theoretic nature: Bruhat--Tits trees. In particular we show how these infinite trees, whose vertices and edges have a very simple representation as two-by-two matrices, have strong connections with both the world of supersingular elliptic curves and the world of quaternion algebras and may then represent a useful tool for doing cryptanalysis in supersingular isogeny graphs.

    This is a joint work with Laia Amorós, Kristin Lauter, Chloe Martindale and Jana Sotáková.

  • 06/12/2019: An application of the Hasse-Weil bound to rational functions over finite fields, Arithmetic, Geometry, Cryptography, and Coding Theory 2019 (AGC^2T-17), CIRM, Marseille, France

    An application of the Hasse-Weil bound to rational functions over finite fields

    The Hasse–Weil bound has many applications in the arithmetic of finite fields. Indeed, in the study of polynomial equations over finite fields, it represents a powerful tool for proving asymptotic claims, i.e. when the cardinality of the finite field is sufficiently large. Examples of such asymptotic results appear, for instance, in the literature about permutation polynomials over finite fields.

    In this talk we will show how we can use the Hasse–Weil bound for proving results about rational functions over finite fields. For instance, given $f(x)$, $g(x)\in \mathbb F_q(x)$, we will discuss the conditions under which there exists $h(x)\in\mathbb F_q(x)$ such that $f(x)=g(h(x))$. This is a joint work with Xiang-dong Hou.

  • 12/11/2018: A note on the security of CSIDH, Indocrypt 2018, New Delhi, India.

    A note on the security of CSIDH

    We propose a quantum algorithm for computing an isogeny between two elliptic curves $E_1,E_2$ defined over a finite field such that there is an imaginary quadratic order $\mathcal O$ satisfying $\mathcal O\simeq \operatorname{End}(E_i)$ for $i = 1,2$. This concerns ordinary curves and supersingular curves defined over $\mathbb F_p$ (the latter used in the recent CSIDH proposal).

    Our algorithm has heuristic asymptotic run time $e^{O\left(\sqrt{\log(|\Delta|)}\right)}$ and requires polynomial quantum memory and $e^{O\left(\sqrt{\log(|\Delta|)}\right)}$ quantumly accessible classical memory, where $\Delta$ is the discriminant of $\mathcal O$. This asymptotic complexity outperforms all other available methods for computing isogenies.

  • 01/10/2020: A quantum attack to the isogeny problem in the "commutative case", Seminar, Centrum Wiskunde & Informatica (CWI), Amsterdam.

    A quantum attack to the isogeny problem in the "commutative case"

    The security of isogeny-based cryptosystems relies on the hardness of the so-called isogeny problem, i.e. the problem of computing an explicit isogeny (a non-constant rational map which is also a group homomorphism) between two elliptic curves. In this talk, after reviewing the fundamental mathematics behind isogeny-based cryptography, we will discuss a quantum attack to the isogeny problem in the "commutative case", that is when the endomorphism rings of the two curves are isomorphic to the same quadratic imaginary order. This applies for instance to CSIDH, a recent proposal by Castryck et al.

    This is a joint work with Jean-François Biasse and Michael J. Jacobson.

    A singular trip through the world of algebraic curves over finite fields

    For an algebraic curve defined over a finite field $\mathbb F_q$, the number of rational points, i.e. of points of the curve with coordinates in $\mathbb F_q$, is always finite.
